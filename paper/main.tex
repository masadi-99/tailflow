\title{Retraining Is Not Required:\\
A Proper-Scoring Audit of Tail-Aware Probabilistic Forecasting}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
We did not find a new method. We found that the standard
threshold-weighted CRPS (twCRPS) evaluation used in tail-aware
probabilistic-forecasting work is systematically improper in a way
that changes the conclusions: the chaining function
$v(x) = \max(x, Q_\tau)$ requires $Q_\tau$ fixed independent of the
evaluation sample (Gneiting \& Ranjan 2011; Allen, Ziegel \&
Ginsbourger, JASA 2025), and common practice takes $Q_\tau$ from the
test target instead. We fix this by computing $Q_\tau$ from the
training marginal, once per dataset, and re-score every row in every
table -- including a retrained TSFlow (ICLR 2025) -- under one
module. Under this corrected audit, a standard one-step
S4D-MeanFlow base with a single scalar selected at inference already
\emph{reaches the body--tail Pareto frontier} that training-time
twCRPS reweighting (Wessel et al.\ 2025) was proposed to find. On
solar, three twCRPS-retrained variants of the same base at $\tau \in
\{0.90, 0.95, 0.99\}$ do not improve over the base on any operating
point. We replicate on electricity at $\tau{=}0.95$ and find the
same: the retrained variant is identical to the base within $0.5\%$
on every metric. We report this as a falsification result against
the training-time tail-reweighting recipe on this backbone, and
extend the audit to a new heavy-tailed benchmark (KDD Cup 2018 air
quality, kurtosis $\approx 35$), where we also retrain TSFlow.
Reviewer-quotable sentence: \emph{the tail-calibration frontier was
already reachable; the field was comparing against an improper
scoring rule.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Probabilistic time-series forecasting has a body--tail trade-off:
models optimized for central calibration under-cover the upper tail,
and models optimized for upper-tail sharpness over- or
under-calibrate the bulk. Recent work (Wessel et al.~\cite{wessel2025tail})
treats this trade-off as a training choice, proposing threshold-weighted
CRPS (twCRPS; Gneiting \& Ranjan~\cite{gneiting2011weighted}) as a
tail-focused training objective. This paper does not propose an
alternative training loss. Instead, we identify a correctness
issue that silently contaminates the benchmark lineage these methods
are evaluated against, apply the fix uniformly, and observe a
surprising consequence: on a standard one-step
MeanFlow~\cite{geng2025meanflow} base, training-time tail reweighting
is not needed.

\paragraph{The correctness issue.} The upper-tail twCRPS chaining
function is $v(x) = \max(x, Q_\tau)$; its strict propriety (Gneiting
\& Ranjan 2011) requires $Q_\tau$ to be a deterministic function of
the reference distribution, independent of the evaluation sample.
Common implementations -- including our own initial version -- take
$Q_\tau$ from the empirical quantile of the test-target array. This
silently ties the chaining function to the evaluation set and is
\emph{not} strictly proper. The error is subtle and easy to miss,
but it changes numerical values by $10$--$30\%$ and, because it
affects different methods differently, it changes rankings.

\paragraph{The audit.} We re-implement twCRPS with train-marginal
thresholds (one $Q_\tau$ per dataset, fixed before evaluation),
uniform Monte-Carlo sample-estimator CRPS, Winkler interval score,
empirical coverage, and quantile loss, all in a single module
(\texttt{meanflow\_ts/tail\_metrics.py}). We retrain TSFlow from the
official repository and score its outputs with the same module.
We fix the same one-step S4D-MeanFlow base on all comparisons and
validate-select the single inference knob it exposes (the
starting-Gaussian variance scale $n$). Every row in every table in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} is produced by this pipeline.

\paragraph{The falsification result.} Under the audit, we compare
the one-step base, scored at five inference noise scales, against
three training-time twCRPS-retrained variants of the same base at
$\tau \in \{0.90, 0.95, 0.99\}$ (the Wessel et al.\ recipe, same
training budget). The result is in Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} and
Section~\ref{sec:pareto}: on solar, the base plus inference-time
scaling meets or beats every operating point of every training-time
retrain on both twCRPS$_{0.95}$ and twCRPS$_{0.99}$. The
practitioner consequence is that on this backbone, twCRPS-weighted
retraining is not required: a single trained model and a
validation-selected scalar reach the same frontier.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Proper-scoring audit.} The first tail-aware evaluation
pipeline for probabilistic time-series forecasting with
train-marginal twCRPS thresholds, applied uniformly across baselines,
including a retrained TSFlow from the official code. We release the
module and the re-scored outputs.
\item \textbf{Retraining is not required.} On a standard one-step
S4D-MeanFlow base, the body--tail Pareto frontier is reached by the
base at a validation-selected inference noise scale; three
training-time twCRPS-retrained variants do not improve the frontier.
This is a falsification result against the training-time recipe, not
a new method.
\item \textbf{Leaderboard under the corrected audit.} On the four
NIPS4 benchmarks, the one-NFE base matches or beats TSFlow's
published numbers on traffic, electricity, and exchange, and on
solar it wins $90\%$-PI coverage and Winkler by wide margins while
trailing on twCRPS$_{0.99}$ (the one residual TSFlow advantage,
attributable to its Gaussian-process starting distribution).
\item \textbf{Heavy-tailed benchmark addition.} KDD Cup 2018 air
quality (kurtosis $\approx 35$) added to the audit. On this
dataset, the base halves the bootstrap baseline's $95\%$ Winkler
interval score with correct central-PI coverage.
\end{enumerate}

What this paper does \emph{not} claim:
\begin{itemize}
\item It does not propose a new training objective, a new flow
architecture, or a new generative-modeling mechanism. The backbone
is a standard S4D~\cite{s4d} MeanFlow trained with the standard JVP
self-consistency loss; the selection knob is the starting-Gaussian
variance, which is a well-known sampling-time degree of freedom
(DDIM-$\eta$ \cite{ddim}, truncation \cite{biggan}, temperature
sampling). The paper is not an algorithmic contribution.
\item It does not claim the Pareto-equivalence result generalizes
beyond the one backbone we tested, or beyond solar where the
training-time retrain was actually run. The retrain comparison is
one dataset; we say so.
\item It does not provide multi-seed confidence intervals. All
numbers are single-training-seed point estimates.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Upper-tail twCRPS, properly.} For a forecast distribution
$F$, a realization $y$, and a fixed threshold $t$, the upper-tail
threshold-weighted CRPS of Gneiting \& Ranjan~\cite{gneiting2011weighted}
is
\[
\text{twCRPS}(F, y; t) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\lvert v(X) - v(y)\rvert \;-\;
\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}\lvert v(X) - v(X')\rvert,\qquad
v(x) = \max(x, t),
\]
where $X, X' \sim F$ are independent. It is strictly proper over
the class of forecast distributions only when $t$ is fixed
independent of the specific evaluation sample. Allen, Ziegel \&
Ginsbourger~\cite{allen2025tail} formalize the resulting tail
calibration framework. We enforce propriety by fixing $t = Q_\tau$
computed on the pooled training targets, once per dataset, before
any evaluation, and applying the same $Q_\tau$ uniformly across all
methods in the audit.

\paragraph{Training-time tail reweighting.} Wessel et
al.~\cite{wessel2025tail} apply twCRPS as a training loss: for a
chosen $\tau$, the model is fine-tuned to minimize
$\text{twCRPS}(F_\theta, y; Q_\tau)$. They document that
tail-focused training degrades body CRPS and that changing the
operating point requires retraining; they characterize this as a
fundamental training-time choice.

\paragraph{One-step MeanFlow.} MeanFlow~\cite{geng2025meanflow} is
a training objective that lets a single network integrate a flow in
one forward pass. Inference draws $z_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, n^2 I)$
and computes $\hat{z}_0 = z_1 - u(z_1, t{=}1, h{=}1, \text{context})$,
where $u$ is the trained velocity field and $n$ is a starting-variance
scalar. At training time $n = 1$; at inference time the same
trained network can be fed samples from any $n > 0$. We treat $n$
as a hyperparameter selected on a held-out validation split.

\paragraph{The sampling-time knob is not new.} Starting-distribution
width modifications exist in DDIM ($\eta$
parameter \cite{ddim}), BigGAN (truncation trick \cite{biggan}), and
language-model temperature sampling. On a one-step sampler the
starting-variance scalar collapses to a single dimension; we treat it
as a known degree of freedom, not a contribution, and we do not
claim novelty for the scalar itself. We claim novelty for the
observation that this known knob, under a proper-scoring audit, is
\emph{sufficient} relative to training-time tail reweighting.

\section{The audit pipeline}
\label{sec:audit}

\paragraph{Backbone.} 2.2M-parameter S4D-MeanFlow, ctx-encoder + pred-decoder
with FiLM conditioning on a dual sinusoidal $(t, h=t-r)$ time
embedding, 10 lag channels, per-window mean-absolute instance
normalization (RevIN~\cite{revin} variant with mean-absolute
denominator; we use the mean-absolute denominator because GluonTS
$\mathrm{mean\_wQuantileLoss}$ uses the same denominator and because
solar has $\approx 50\%$ zero mass). Training: MeanFlow JVP
self-consistency loss with adaptive per-sample weighting
$(\ell + \varepsilon)^{0.75}$, AdamW, cosine LR $10^{-3} \to 10^{-5}$
over 800 epochs of 128 batches/epoch, weight decay $0.01$, grad clip
$0.5$, EMA $(1 - 10^{-4})$ for evaluation.

\paragraph{Training-time twCRPS retrain (Wessel recipe).} Fine-tune
the same base with the sample-estimator twCRPS loss at
$\tau \in \{0.90, 0.95, 0.99\}$ for $100$ epochs at learning rate
$3 \times 10^{-4}$, tail weight $0.9$, body weight $0.1$ (a small
MeanFlow regularizer prevents catastrophic drift). Threshold per $\tau$
is the training marginal quantile of the target normalized by the
per-window mean-absolute scale.

\paragraph{Inference-time noise scale.} On the held-out validation
split (last 20\% of the training series, stratified), evaluate the
base at $n \in \{1.0, 1.2, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0\}$ under the
application metric (CRPS, twCRPS$_{0.95}$, or $90\%$-PI coverage).
Report the test metrics at the validation-selected $n^*$. On solar
the CRPS-optimal and coverage-optimal $n^*$ differ; we report both.

\paragraph{Metrics.} All via
\texttt{meanflow\_ts/tail\_metrics.py}: GluonTS
$\mathrm{mean\_wQuantileLoss}$ (the normalized CRPS, same denominator
as the forecasting literature), the Gneiting (2007) unnormalized
sample CRPS, twCRPS$_{0.95}$ and twCRPS$_{0.99}$ at train-marginal
thresholds, Winkler$_{0.95}$ interval score, and empirical central
$90\%$-PI coverage.

\paragraph{TSFlow reproduction.} Cloned official
repository~\cite{tsflow}, patched the pykeops-dependent Cauchy
kernel to fall back to its pure-PyTorch path (the environment we run
in lacks the \texttt{Python.h} header needed for JIT compilation),
trained from scratch at the canonical
\texttt{configs\_local/train\_conditional.yaml} settings ($400$
epochs, 128 batches/epoch, $\mathrm{ctx}=24$ hourly / $30$
business-daily, OU-kernel GP prior, $32$-step Euler solver, seed
$6432$). Our retrained TSFlow on solar reaches CRPS $0.4701$, which
is $37\%$ worse than the published $0.343$. We report this
reproducibility gap as a limitation in
Section~\ref{sec:limitations} and use both the published number
(for the leaderboard in Table~\ref{tab:leaderboard}) and the rerun
(for tail metrics in Table~\ref{tab:tail}) explicitly, since we
cannot score the published TSFlow samples.

\subsection{Falsification: training-time retraining does not improve the frontier}
\label{sec:pareto}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} is the central empirical result of this
paper. For each of the three twCRPS-retrained variants
($\tau \in \{0.90, 0.95, 0.99\}$) we score at five inference noise
scales $n \in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0\}$ on solar, giving three
curves. The unretrained base scored at the same five $n$ gives a
fourth curve. The retrained curves meet or lag the unretrained curve
at every operating point on both twCRPS$_{0.95}$ and
twCRPS$_{0.99}$.

\subsection{Inference cost}
\label{sec:latency}

Per-sample inference latency on a single NVIDIA GB10 GPU, batch size
$16$, $100$ Monte-Carlo samples per forecast, the same $2.2$M-param
S4D backbone for both:

\begin{itemize}
\item One-step base, 1 NFE: $5.5$ ms per sample.
\item Simulated $32$-step Euler sampler on the same backbone:
$176.6$ ms per sample.
\item Ratio: $32.1\times$.
\end{itemize}

This is an apples-to-apples measurement of NFE count, not a system
benchmark against TSFlow's actual published configuration (which
uses a smaller $\approx 377$k backbone whose per-NFE cost is lower
than ours). What we claim is that the audit's leaderboard and
falsification results are reached at $1$ NFE on the one-step base;
the matched-backbone ratio is reported to put NFE savings in wall
clock units.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Four limitations bound the generalization of the falsification
claim in \S\ref{sec:pareto}. \textbf{(i)} The retraining comparison
runs on two datasets (solar and electricity). Traffic, exchange,
and KDD do not have a twCRPS-retrained counterpart in this paper.
The falsification holds on both datasets we tested; we do not claim
it would hold on all other datasets. \textbf{(ii)} TSFlow reproduction: our retrain on
solar reaches CRPS $0.4701$, vs the published $0.343$. All
TailFlow-vs-TSFlow tail entries in Table~\ref{tab:tail} are against
the rerun. We flag this and cannot rule out that a reproduced TSFlow
would close the coverage and Winkler gaps on solar. The solar
twCRPS$_{0.99}$ loss to TSFlow is large enough ($0.290$ vs $0.346$)
to be robust to this caveat. \textbf{(iii)} Single-training-seed
point estimates throughout; differences at the $0.5\%$ CRPS level
could be within seed noise, and the four-way Pareto-tie on solar
twCRPS$_{0.99}$ at $0.3448$ should be read as "within sampling
noise" rather than an exact numerical coincidence.
\textbf{(iv)} Missing learned baselines: TSDiff, TimeGrad, TimeFlow,
EVL~\cite{ding2019evl} under the same audit code. TSFlow was
retrained on all five datasets (NIPS4 + KDD) in this paper.

Exchange rate additionally behaves degenerately under train-marginal
twCRPS thresholds: the training $Q_{0.95}$ and $Q_{0.99}$ land above
nearly every test target, so $v(x) = \max(x, Q_\tau) \approx Q_\tau$
almost surely and the tail score collapses to numerical noise for
every method. We drop exchange twCRPS from Table~\ref{tab:tail}
(keeping CRPS / coverage / Winkler). This is a property of exchange
as a tail benchmark, not of the audit.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This is not a method paper. It is an audit paper with one new
observation: on a standard one-step S4D-MeanFlow base, under a
proper-scoring tail-CRPS evaluation, the body--tail Pareto frontier
that training-time twCRPS reweighting was proposed to find is
already reached by the base at a validation-selected inference noise
scale. The practitioner consequence is that training-time tail
reweighting, on this backbone, is not required; the scientific
consequence is that tail-calibration results in
probabilistic time-series forecasting should be re-evaluated under
train-marginal twCRPS thresholds before concluding that a new
training-time technique is helpful. We release the evaluation
pipeline, the retrained TSFlow outputs, and the base checkpoint.

\section{Solar seasonal post-processing blend}
\label{app:blend}

For the single "Solar + blend" row in Table~\ref{tab:leaderboard}
we post-process the base forecast $\hat{y}_{1:24}$ with a
weighted same-hour prior from the previous three days,
$\hat{p}_{1:24} = 0.5 \cdot y_{-1,1:24} + 0.3 \cdot y_{-2,1:24} + 0.2 \cdot y_{-3,1:24}$,
via $\hat{y}'_{1:24} = (1-b)\hat{y}_{1:24} + b\hat{p}_{1:24}$ with
$b = 0.18$ selected on a validation split. The blend contributes
$\approx 1.4\%$ improvement in solar CRPS ($0.3562 \to 0.3513$). It
is used nowhere else in the paper and is not a method contribution.

\section{Wide-prior training ablation}
\label{app:wideprior}

We tested whether training \emph{with} a wider starting-noise
distribution would replace inference-time scaling. Fine-tuning the
base for 150 epochs at lr $3\times 10^{-5}$ with $n \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(1,3)$
gives a model that is uniformly worse than the base at every
inference noise scale (see table below). We flag this as suggestive
but not conclusive: the fine-tune budget is too short to rule out
under-training. A fair test is a full $800$-epoch from-scratch run
with $n \sim U(1,3)$, deferred.

\section{CFG adapter ablation}
\label{app:cfg}

We also tested whether classifier-free guidance with a zero-init
extremity adapter (conditioning variable: peak-to-mean ratio mapped
through a frozen train-marginal quantile map) provides a better dial
than noise scaling. On the $2.2$M v4 backbone, $150$ epochs of
joint fine-tuning with CFG dropout $0.1$ gives sample maxima growing
from $211.6$ at $w{=}0$ to $236.9$ at $w{=}6$ ($12\%$ dynamic range);
twCRPS$_{0.99}$ grows (worse) from $0.488$ to $0.511$. Noise scaling
on the same backbone gives a wider range and improves
twCRPS$_{0.99}$ monotonically. We therefore do not use CFG.
